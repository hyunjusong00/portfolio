Before I had a PM title, I had a 30-person student team, a broken editing tool from 2014, and a website that hid the design team's best work behind a wall of text. The year I spent as Editor-in-Chief at the Kookmin University Newspaper was the first time I did the parts of product work that don't show up in a PM job description.

## What I joined

I started on the design team. The newspaper itself was bigger than people assume — 30+ student journalists, editors, and designers, with a real production cadence and a real campus audience.

Two things stood out from day one:

- **The team was internationally mixed.** Korean and international students worked side by side with different design backgrounds and different vocabularies for visual feedback. Without a shared system, consistency depended on one person centralizing review.
- **The tooling was from another era.** Reporting and design only had one place to collaborate: a custom editing tool a senior had built for the newspaper a decade earlier. It worked, but it forced everyone into the workflow it was designed for in 2014, not the one the team needed in 2024.

## What I noticed but couldn't fix yet

As a design team member, I could feel both costs but couldn't act on them.

Because feedback vocabularies varied, design consistency bottlenecked through one reviewer. Because the editing tool was slow, every reporting → design handoff took longer than it should have. And because the newspaper's website hadn't been redesigned in years, the visual work the design team poured hours into showed up at half its quality once it was online — fifteen designers shipping into a surface that hid their work.

I kept the list in my head and waited for the seat that would let me touch it.

## Two bets I made when I became Editor-in-Chief

I had limited time and a lot of inherited problems. I picked two bets and committed to them.

**Bet 1 — Replace the internal communication tooling so the two teams could actually collaborate.**

The old custom editor was the biggest single source of friction, but I didn't start there. I started with the leadership team: I migrated us to **Notion** for anything structured (editorial calendars, role assignments, archives) and **KakaoWork** for anything synchronous. Two unglamorous decisions that ended up being load-bearing.

The downstream effect was bigger than I expected. Separating *work* from *personal chat*, clarifying who owned what, and giving people a written record of decisions meant fewer arguments about ambiguity. Internal friction dropped almost immediately.

**Bet 2 — Rebuild the public website so the design team's work would be the front door.**

The old site treated articles as text-first, with design as decoration. I wanted to flip that — make the visual identity of each piece part of what readers actually saw on the page. We worked with the school's IT department and the information administration team for a month, studying how other university newsrooms and public news outlets solved the same problem. We rebuilt navigation around how readers actually browsed — by issue, by visual hook — instead of forcing articles into the old CMS's rigid columns.

## What shipped, measured

After a semester:

- **Website traffic up 40%+** in Google Analytics.
- **Instagram followers grew 1.5×**, ~200 new followers in one semester.
- Feedback from professors, the dean, and the student body shifted from neutral to genuinely warm.

The follower growth came from the other quiet bet: the design team started shooting and editing **video Reels** for stories that had only ever been published as articles. Design moved from a downstream service into its own publishing surface.

## What I'd take into product work

Looking back, this role was the first time I did the parts of product work that don't show up in a PM job description.

- **Tools shape behavior.** Migrating the leadership chat changed the team's *relationships*, not just its workflow. Tooling decisions feel like logistics. They aren't.
- **Visibility is leverage.** A team doing great work into an invisible surface gets demoralized fast. Sometimes the highest-leverage move is changing the surface, not the work.
- **Cross-functional fit ≠ same role.** International and Korean students didn't need to do the work the same way. They needed shared vocabulary at the *handoff*, and freedom inside their own scope.
- **Some bets only look obvious in hindsight.** Replacing the editor and redesigning the website were "obvious." Doing them required permission, time, and a willingness to break what was already there. Most product bets I've made since have had the same shape.

I didn't have the title "Product Manager" yet. But a lot of how I think now started in that newsroom.
