## The problem

At pre-seed, founders and investors are looking for the same thing from opposite directions. Founders want fast, structured signal on whether an idea is worth pursuing — before they spend three months building. Investors want to see ideas before they're packaged into decks, when conviction is still soft and the founder is still talking to anyone.

Existing tools sit at the extremes. ChatGPT-style validators hand you a single score with no consequences attached. McKinsey-style market reports take weeks and cost more than the validation is worth. AngelList shows you companies that already exist. Nothing fills the gap where the actual decision happens.

The first version of Trywebb tried to fill that gap with peer feedback — Software students reviewing each other's projects. The hypothesis was builders helping builders. The data made it clear within a month: of the first 30 sign-ups, four came back after week one. The most frequent comment on submissions was "nice UI." Students reviewing students produced encouragement, not signal.

## My role

PM for a team of four — one developer, two marketers, and me. I own product direction, the AI evaluation architecture, the pivot calls, and a meaningful slice of the frontend code (Next.js / React).

Picking up Git and the React stack mid-build changed how the team worked. Specs became components instead of "wait, that's not what I meant" loops. It also forced every product decision through one filter — *is this worth three days of someone else's life?* Pure PMs sometimes forget to ask that.

## Key decisions

### Pivoted from peer feedback to a two-sided market

The 4-out-of-30 retention curve didn't lie. Builders helping builders is a nice idea; it's not a market. Trywebb pivoted to founders × pre-seed VCs. Both sides have skin in the game, and each side solves the other's cold-start problem — investors subscribe because there's pre-product deal flow they can't get elsewhere, founders submit because someone with capital might actually be reading.

The pivot wasn't panic. It was retention numbers + seven angel-investor conversations over six weeks + the honest acceptance that the original framing didn't survive contact with users.

### Cut the analysis system from 8 agents to 6

The MVP shipped with 8 evaluation agents because eight angles felt thorough. After sitting with those angel investors and going deep into how YC and a16z actually evaluate ideas (Sequoia BP template, Thiel's seven questions, NFX on network effects), I cut to 6:

`market_size` · `problem_urgency` · `timing` · `product` · `defensibility` · `business_model`

What got cut, and why:

- **Regulation** → not a standalone axis; absorbed into business-model viability.
- **Competition** → absorbed into defensibility (Thiel: "competition is for losers").
- **User acquisition** → CAC belongs inside business-model unit economics; "acquisition strategy" alone is meaningless without the rest.
- **Technical difficulty** → renamed to *product*, reframed from "is this hard to build" to "is this 10× better."

More always feels safer when you don't know who you're building for. The cut was obvious in week six and would have been impossible in week one.

### Re-architected the analysis pipeline for speed

The original three-pass per agent (Draft → Skeptic → Judge) was 50+ seconds end-to-end. Two changes brought it to ~30s without losing analytical depth:

1. **Skeptic merged into Judge.** The Judge prompt now does internal critique in Step 1 and reconciliation in Step 2 — same scoring weights (60% skeptic / 40% draft), one fewer LLM call per agent.
2. **Tavily search parallelized with Draft generation.** Draft no longer waits for evidence; Judge integrates it during reconciliation. Saves 2–3 seconds per agent.

Plus removed the redundant LLM quality gate (the heuristic gate already catches keyboard mash) and the synthesizer-critique pass (it almost never produced changes the agent layer hadn't already made).

### Built the frontend myself

Specs that would have taken a week of Figma → meeting → developer → revision rounds shipped in an afternoon. The bigger effect was filtering — when you have to write the component, *is this worth it?* becomes a real question, not a stylistic one.

A concrete example: the dashboard had a right-rail with stats, recent activity, and quick-action shortcuts. Sitting in the code, it became obvious that the quick-actions panel was 100% duplicate of the sidebar nav. Killed it. The stats panel was abstract numbers nobody would act on. Replaced it with a horizontal strip showing *Last submission · Best signal · Reports complete.* The page got cleaner, the dashboard got sharper, and no committee discussion was required.

## What's shipped

**Two-sided product surface**
- Founder side: submit through 6 axis-specific structured questions, receive a signal report in ~30s, iterate up to 3 times per idea, optional public profile (`/u/[id]`) with bio + social links.
- Investor side (Pro): curated market view across 9 categories, watchlist with social-proof signal (*N investors watching*), direct-intro flow.

**6-agent AI analysis pipeline**
- 6 axes, each scored independently by a Draft → Judge two-pass agent.
- Tavily-backed external evidence retrieval per axis, parallelized with Draft.
- Synthesizer layer producing a composite score via weighted arithmetic mean (the original harmonic mean over-penalized any single weak axis).
- Module-scope prompt cache + lazy quality gate: end-to-end ~30s.

**Category-specific prompt overlay**
- 9 categories (SaaS, Consumer, Fintech, Health, Hardware, etc.) with per-axis customizations layered on a SaaS base — the Health `market_size` agent weights reimbursement; the Fintech `timing` agent weights regulatory rule changes.

**Three-tier pricing**
- Free / Plus $10 / Pro $20, with explicit role mapping (Founders → Free / Plus, Investors → Pro) and a *For Founders / For Investors* toggle on `/pricing` instead of one shared menu.

**Pre-launch GTM**
- Reddit-based discovery experiments running pre-launch.
- 6 user interviews (founders + aspiring investors / market researchers) lined up before public release.

## What this taught me

That the answer to *what should I cut?* is always obvious once you know who the user is — and almost always invisible until then. The 8-to-6 agent decision was easy in week six and would have been impossible in week one.

That picking up unfamiliar code (React, Git workflows) as a PM changes how the whole team operates — but only if you actually ship, not just demo.

That two-sided markets are mostly a sequencing problem, not a feature problem. The wrong order kills you. The right order makes both sides bootstrap each other.
