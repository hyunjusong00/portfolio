## The problem

@kmu_bizon, the newspaper's Instagram account, was treated the way most institutional Instagram accounts are: a place to repost finished articles. Each post was a static image with the headline, sometimes a pull quote, sometimes a photo from the print edition. Engagement was low and growth was flat. Followers were mostly people who already knew the paper from print or campus.

The structural issue was simple. **Instagram in 2024 was a video-first feed.** Reels were eating both the algorithm's recommendation surface and the actual viewing time of every demographic we wanted to reach — and we were posting still images of text. We were a text-first paper publishing into a video-first feed.

The choice was either accept being invisible there, or build a video pipeline. We picked the second.

## My role

Editor-in-Chief. I defined the editorial direction — what kind of stories became Reels, what angle each one took, how the narrative beats and visual tone laddered up to the paper's broader voice. The design team did the shooting and editing. The handoff between us was the part I personally spent the most time designing.

## Key decisions

### Reels as a parallel publishing channel, not a marketing afterthought

The first decision was treating Reels with the same editorial seriousness as articles. That meant:

- Every Reel had a **brief written before anything was shot** — angle, beats, tone, what the viewer should feel by second 3 / second 7 / end.
- Every Reel had a **named editor** responsible for it, the same way articles do.
- We tracked Reels in the editorial calendar alongside print pieces, so Reels production didn't get continuously deprioritized whenever a print deadline pressed.

This was the only decision that mattered. Most institutional accounts fail at video because they treat it as marketing-team output. We treated it as journalism in a different format, which is what it actually was.

### Script-before-shoot, not shoot-and-cut

The temptation with Reels is to shoot a lot and edit something usable out of the footage. We did the opposite. Every Reel started as a **30–45 second written script** with shot direction and approximate timing. The design team shot to that script.

Two reasons. First, our shooters were students with class schedules, not full-time videographers — we couldn't afford to shoot for hours hoping for a take. Second, scripting first forced the editorial logic to hold up before any production effort went in. About one in four scripts didn't survive the table read. Those Reels never got shot, which was the point.

### Defined topic angles, narrative beats, visual tone — then handed off

I wrote three pieces of brief for every Reel before handing it to design:

1. **Topic angle.** Not the topic — the *angle* on the topic. "The new dining hall" was a topic; "what students who eat alone there make of the new layout" was an angle.
2. **Narrative beats.** Hook (sec 0–3), tension (sec 3–7), payoff (sec 7–end). Mapped to the same three-act muscle articles use.
3. **Visual tone.** Two or three reference frames pulled from existing Reels in adjacent spaces (campus journalism, magazine accounts, design-led documentary style) showing the *feel* I wanted, not the literal shots.

Once the design team had those three pieces, I stayed out of execution. Their footage and edits were better than anything I would have specified at the storyboard level.

### Built a cadence, not just hits

We committed to a steady rhythm of Reels — not viral spikes. The premise was that a follower who's seen four good Reels from us in a row will start opening the fifth without checking who posted it, and that compounding habit is what builds an account, not any single hit.

We were right about that, and wrong about how many Reels it took. The cadence took longer to lock in than I expected — closer to seven or eight in a row before the watch-time on new posts started to climb on its own.

## What shipped

- Reels editorial system: written brief template, named-editor model, calendar integration with print
- A library of reusable script structures (campus interview, on-location explainer, behind-the-scenes process, micro-investigative)
- Production handoff — angle, beats, visual tone — that let the design team execute without micromanagement
- Steady weekly Reels output through the semester

## What it actually moved

- **Instagram followers grew 1.5×** within one semester
- About **200 new followers in a single semester**, almost all of it driven by Reels
- The design team's range expanded — they were now publishing video, not just print spreads

## What this taught me

That **most "social media for institutions" advice is bad** because it treats the platform as a distribution channel for content that already exists. If we'd done that, we'd have stayed invisible. The platform was the format constraint; the work was reshaping what we made to fit it.

That **briefing well is most of the design management job.** Three pieces of brief — angle, beats, tone — shipped better Reels than detailed storyboards would have. The design team owned execution; I owned the editorial frame they executed inside.

That **a team that hasn't done video before will be cautious for the first six or seven attempts and then suddenly get bolder.** Whatever creative skill the campus design team built in that semester compounds for whoever leads the paper next, which was always the goal underneath the metric.
