## The problem

The Kookmin University Newspaper had been publishing weekly for years and had a 15-person design team putting real hours into every issue's visual identity. Almost none of that work made it to the website. The site was a mid-2010s university CMS — text-first, image-as-thumbnail, navigation by rigid category buckets ("Campus / Society / Culture / Opinion") that didn't reflect how anyone actually read the paper.

The cost was twofold. Readers landing on the site couldn't tell what was worth reading at a glance — every article looked identical until you clicked into it. And the design team, whose work was the strongest part of every print issue, was effectively invisible online.

When I became Editor-in-Chief, that gap was the first thing I committed to closing.

## My role

Editor-in-Chief of a 30+ person team. I owned the redesign end to end: defining the strategic intent, leading the cross-functional collaboration with the school's IT department and information administration team, running the design direction, and signing off on every IA and visual decision before launch. The design team I'd previously been part of did the execution; I held the editorial line.

## Key decisions

### Design as the front door, not decoration

The framing decision behind every other decision: *the visual identity of each article is part of what the reader sees on the page, not a thumbnail bolted onto a text block.* That meant every article got a custom hero treatment — typography tuned to the piece, layout shaped to its rhythm, image and color choices that signaled tone before the headline did.

It was a more expensive editorial process per issue. It was also the only way to make the design team's work readable at the URL.

### Restructured information architecture around reading behavior

The old IA was a category tree inherited from the print edition, which had inherited it from a layout grid from the 2000s. It didn't match how readers actually navigated.

We watched what readers did on the existing site (analytics + a small qualitative round with 8 students) and rebuilt navigation around two behaviors:

- **Browsing by issue.** Readers who came back regularly wanted to see what was *new this week*, not drill down by category. The new homepage led with the current issue as a curated cover, the way a magazine would.
- **Browsing by visual hook.** First-time readers (most of our long-tail traffic) didn't browse by topic — they grazed. The new feed surfaced visually-led entry points instead of text headlines, with image, dek, and tone cues before category labels.

Categories didn't disappear; they moved from primary navigation to a secondary filter, where they belong.

### Cross-functional collaboration with school IT

The newspaper's site sits inside the university's broader portal infrastructure — we couldn't redesign in isolation. I ran a month of weekly working sessions with the school's IT department and information administration team, learning what could and couldn't be changed in the existing CMS, what custom templates we could ship, and what compliance constraints (accessibility, archival linking) we had to respect.

The patient institutional work — meetings, getting the right people in the room, translating editorial intent into IT-feasible specs — was the part of the job nobody warned me about and the part that mattered most. The redesign would have been impossible without it.

### Researched real comparison points

Before any visual decision, we studied:

- **Other Korean university newsrooms** — to see what was possible inside similar institutional constraints.
- **Public news outlets with strong web design** — *The Pudding,* *The Atlantic's CityLab,* *Hankyoreh's longform features* — for the editorial-design-led pattern we were trying to bring to a campus paper.

Most of what we built wasn't original. What was original was being the first campus paper around us to take the idea of *editorial design online* seriously.

## What shipped

- Full IA redesign: issue-led homepage, visual feed, secondary category filtering
- Custom article templates with editorial-design-led hero treatments
- Modernized typographic system, improved photo treatment, mobile-first responsive layout
- Accessibility and archive compatibility maintained throughout
- Cross-functional working agreement with school IT for ongoing iteration

## What it actually moved

- **Website traffic up 40%+** within one semester (Google Analytics)
- Faculty, the dean, and student body feedback shifted from neutral to genuinely warm
- Internal: the design team's morale changed. Their work was finally landing where readers were

## What this taught me

That **the most expensive part of any "design redesign" is the institutional work,** not the design. The month of meetings with school IT was the redesign — the visual decisions came easily after that.

That **information architecture is editorial work disguised as engineering.** Deciding to put the current issue ahead of the category tree was a stronger editorial statement than any headline change.

That **a team's morale tracks the surface their work ships into.** The design team had been doing great work for years. What changed wasn't the team; it was finally building the surface that made the work visible. I think about this every time I touch a product surface now.
